# Operations — What Running This Actually Looks Like Day to Day

> Daycare and boarding are two different businesses under one roof. Understanding the operational difference is critical before committing to either or both.

---

## Daycare vs. Boarding: What's Actually Different

| Dimension | Daycare | Boarding (Kennel) |
|---|---|---|
| **When dogs are there** | Drop off AM, pick up PM — dogs go home at night | Dogs sleep there overnight, multiple nights |
| **Revenue model** | Per-day fee; recurring customers are the lifeblood | Per-night fee; spikes around holidays/travel seasons |
| **Space needed** | Large open play areas, minimal sleeping space | Individual kennel runs with sleeping area per dog |
| **Staffing intensity** | High — active supervision during all play hours | Lower overnight; higher during day if daycare included |
| **Licensing complexity** | Slightly simpler | Full GDA kennel license required |
| **Risk profile** | Dog-on-dog incidents during play | Dog escapes, illness spread, medical emergencies overnight |
| **Customer relationship** | Weekly/daily regulars — deep relationship | Episodic — travelers, vacation-season spikes |
| **Seasonality** | Relatively even year-round | Peaks: Thanksgiving, Christmas, summer vacations |

**The smart play at 14,612 SF:** Run both. Boarding dogs participate in daycare during the day, which means the space earns daycare revenue during day hours AND boarding revenue overnight. This is the Dogtopia model and it's efficient.

## What a Typical Day Looks Like

### Daycare-Only Operation (Simpler Starting Point)

| Time | Activity | Staff Needed |
|---|---|---|
| 6:30 AM | Facility prep: clean play areas, fill water stations, check equipment | 1–2 |
| 7:00 AM | Doors open — morning drop-off rush begins | 2–3 |
| 7:00–9:30 AM | Check-in each dog (temperament screen, vaccination check, intake notes) | 2–3 |
| 9:30 AM–12:00 PM | Active play supervision, rotate playgroups, enrichment activities | 2–3 |
| 12:00–1:00 PM | Rest period / midday nap time (dogs need downtime) | 1–2 |
| 1:00–4:30 PM | Afternoon play, grooming appointments happen during this window | 2–3 |
| 4:30–7:00 PM | Evening pick-up rush, checkout each dog | 2–3 |
| 7:00–7:30 PM | Final cleaning, facility lockdown | 1–2 |

**Peak stress moments:** Morning drop-off (7–9:30 AM) and evening pickup (4:30–6:30 PM). These windows need maximum staffing.

### With Boarding Added

| Additional Time | Activity | Staff Needed |
|---|---|---|
| 6:00 AM | Overnight staff wakes boarding dogs, morning potty breaks | 1 overnight |
| 7:00 AM | Boarding dogs join daycare playgroups | Covered above |
| 7:00 PM | Evening check-in for new overnight arrivals | 1 |
| 7:00–10:00 PM | Boarding dogs: evening potty, feeding, kennel settling | 1–2 |
| 10:00 PM–6:00 AM | Overnight monitoring — someone must be on-site or on-call | 1 on-site OR camera monitoring with on-call |

**Key decision:** Full overnight staffing vs. camera-monitored nights. Many facilities do camera monitoring with an on-call staff member. Full overnight staffing adds ~$50K–$80K/year in labor cost.

## Staffing Model

### Staff Roles

| Role | Count | Annual Salary Est. | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Facility Manager / GM** | 1 | $55,000–$75,000 | Runs operations, scheduling, customer relations, compliance |
| **Lead Dog Handler / Trainer** | 1–2 | $38,000–$50,000 | Temperament evaluation, playgroup management, training upsells |
| **Dog Handler (Floor Staff)** | 4–6 | $30,000–$38,000 | Day-to-day play supervision, feeding, cleaning |
| **Groomer** | 1–2 | $35,000–$50,000 | OR commission-based (40–50% of grooming revenue) |
| **Receptionist / Client Services** | 1–2 | $30,000–$38,000 | Check-in/out, phones, booking, upsells |
| **Overnight Attendant** | 1 | $28,000–$35,000 | If you do full overnight staffing |

**Total FTE at stabilized operation: 8–12 people**
**Total annual payroll: ~$380K–$580K** (before benefits and payroll taxes)

### Staff-to-Dog Ratios (Industry Standards)

| Setting | Ratio | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Active group play | 1 staff : 10 dogs (max) | IBPSA (International Boarding & Pet Services Assoc.) |
| Active group play (best practice) | 1 staff : 8 dogs | Industry recommendation |
| Rest/nap periods | 1 staff : 20 dogs | Lower supervision needed |
| Minimum at any time | 2 staff | Never 1 person alone with active playgroup |

**What this means for our facility:**
- If we have 80 dogs in daycare, we need **8–10 floor staff on duty simultaneously**
- That's your peak labor cost moment
- Scheduling in shifts to cover 7 AM–7 PM = 12-hour operating window

### Part-Time vs. Full-Time Strategy
Many facilities run a hybrid:
- 2–3 FT core staff (manager, lead handler)
- 4–8 PT staff filling shift slots
- PT allows flexibility without committing to full benefits load
- Downside: training, consistency, turnover risk

## Operational Challenges to Plan For

### 1. Dog Temperament Screening (Non-Negotiable)
Every new dog must be evaluated before joining group play. This is both a safety and liability issue.

**Standard process:**
- "Meet & greet" or "trial day" — observe dog's behavior with other dogs
- Screening criteria: social behavior, aggression triggers, size compatibility
- Some dogs will fail and cannot attend group daycare (they become boarding-only or are turned away)
- Plan for ~10–15% of dogs to not pass group play screening

### 2. Playgroup Management
Dogs cannot just be dumped in one big room. Best practice:
- **Separate by size**: Small dogs (<20 lbs), medium, large
- **Separate by play style**: Rough players vs. gentler dogs
- **Rotate groups**: Prevents overstimulation and fatigue
- **Rest periods**: Industry standard is mandatory 2-hour rest midday

### 3. Illness Management
Dog daycare is a petri dish. Kennel cough, parasites, and giardia spread fast.
- Vaccination requirements are your first line of defense (Rabies, DA2PP, Bordetella, Canine Influenza)
- Sick dogs must be isolated immediately and sent home
- Deep cleaning protocols after any illness outbreak
- Have a vet relationship established (and listed on your GDA license)

### 4. Incident Reports
Every dog bite, injury, or unusual incident must be documented. This protects you legally and is required for insurance.

### 5. Software (You Need This From Day 1)
Running a dog daycare manually is chaos. Industry-standard software:
- **Gingr** — most popular, ~$200-500/month; handles reservations, check-in, vaccination tracking, invoicing
- **PetExec** — similar; slightly older UI but robust
- **Revelation Pets** — used by The Dog Yard ATL; strong for boarding operations
- Features needed: online booking, automated vaccination expiry alerts, customer profiles, daily report cards, payment processing

### 6. Webcams
This is table stakes in 2025. Customers expect to check on their dogs.
- Live webcam access builds trust and reduces "how's my dog doing?" calls
- Multiple cameras in each play area + rest areas
- DogPhoria and most competitors already do this
- Cost: $500–$2,000 upfront hardware; streaming via Verkada, Nest, or equivalent

## Key Operational KPIs to Track

| KPI | Target | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Average daily dog count | 50+ (ramp to 80) | Core revenue driver |
| Boarding occupancy rate | 40%+ | Utilization metric |
| Customer return rate | 70%+ monthly | Stickiness / loyalty |
| Average revenue per dog | $45+/day (blended with add-ons) | Mix shift indicator |
| Staff turnover rate | <30%/year | Industry avg is 40-50%; beat it |
| Incident rate | <1 per 500 dog-days | Safety metric |
| Vaccination compliance | 100% | Regulatory + insurance |
